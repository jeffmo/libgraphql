libgraphql Design Storybook v9

Design reference for the libgraphql badge, glyph, and banner system. This document captures the rationale, constraints, visual vocabulary, and per-glyph design decisions that define the brand identity across all five crates in the workspace.

1. Design Philosophy

"Graph" is already in "GraphQL." The Graph-Edge Polygons system builds each crate's badge from vertices and straight-line edges — the fundamental primitives of graph theory. Every glyph is a wireframe polygon with visible vertex dots, evoking engineering diagrams and schematic blueprints rather than consumer product logos.

The system is designed for a Rust library ecosystem targeting developers who value precision, minimalism, and technical rigor. The visual language deliberately avoids fills, gradients, and rounded forms in favor of sharp geometry and deliberate sparseness.

Core Principles

Complexity = Scope

Vertex count scales with the crate's functional scope. The simplest crate gets the fewest vertices; the apex crate gets the most.

One Accent Per Glyph

Each glyph has exactly one visual accent that encodes its role. No glyph borrows another's signature element.

Monochrome Amber

All glyphs use a single color: #d4943c. Differentiation comes from shape and structure, not color.

Wireframe Aesthetic

No filled polygons. Strokes and dots only. The shapes should feel like they were drawn on graph paper or a technical schematic.

Accent Vocabulary

Each glyph's unique accent serves as a semantic marker. These accents are exclusive — no two glyphs share the same one.

  • Interior edges (libgraphql) — rich interconnections; the crate ties everything together
  • Ghost vertex + gap (parser) — an open entry point that accepts streaming input. The glyph reads left-to-right: a single "token" node (the ghost vertex) is accepted and grown into a dimensional structure (the solid right edge)
  • Center dot (core) — the engine nucleus at the heart of the system
  • Nested shapes (macros) — code that generates code; compile-time expansion
  • Directional silhouette (cli) — the flying V points toward output/delivery

Directional Bookends

The parser and CLI glyphs form directional bookends of a processing pipeline. The parser opens to the left (receiving input text), and the CLI points to the right (delivering output to the user). Together they frame a flow: text → parser → core → cli → user.

4. Glyph Catalog

libgraphql

hexagon 6 vertices interior diagonals glyph v2

The apex crate that re-exports and unifies the entire ecosystem. Its hexagon carries the highest vertex count, representing maximum scope and complexity. Three interior diagonals form a hexagram pattern, symbolizing the rich interconnections between all sub-crates.

Design Decisions

  • Why hexagon (6)? Provides full bilateral symmetry on both horizontal and vertical axes. The original v1 heptagon (7) felt unbalanced due to odd vertex count lacking bilateral symmetry. Hexagon was the natural step down.
  • Why interior diagonals? They represent the "wiring" between sub-crates — libgraphql doesn't just contain the others, it connects them. The 3 cross-edges at reduced opacity (0.25 in glyph, 0.50 in light banners) form a secondary visual layer beneath the primary polygon.
  • Why no center dot? The center dot is core's exclusive accent. Adding it to the hexagon would make libgraphql look like "bigger core" rather than its own distinct symbol. The interior diagonals already communicate "encompasses everything" without borrowing core's identity.
  • Pointy-top orientation: Places a single vertex at top-center, giving the shape a stable, upward-facing posture appropriate for the root crate.

libgraphql-parser

triangle 3 edges ghost vertex left-opening glyph v5

The parser is the entry point of the system — it receives raw GraphQL text and produces structured AST. Its glyph is the simplest shape (triangle, 3 edges) with a deliberate gap on the left side representing the "mouth" that accepts streaming input.

Design Decisions

  • Why triangle (3)? The parser is the most focused crate — it does one thing (parse). Fewest vertices = narrowest scope. The triangle is also the simplest polygon, mirroring parsing as the first/simplest step.
  • Why left-opening? Text flows left-to-right. The parser faces the direction of incoming input, like a funnel. This was changed from top-opening (v1–v2) to left-opening (v3+) to better convey the streaming input metaphor.
  • Why ghost vertex? The left vertex is rendered at low opacity (0.3) and the edges stop short of reaching it, creating a visible gap. This says "I'm not closed — I accept input." The ghost vertex is the only intentionally-incomplete element in any glyph.
  • Solid right edge: The fully-closed right side represents the structured, complete AST that the parser outputs.
  • Mouth gap tuning: The gap between the two edge tips went through three iterations: 10px (v3, too tight/lipless), 14px (v4, too wide/yawning), 12px (v5, balanced). The final 12px gap is ~27% of the right edge length (44px), large enough to read at 16px favicon size but small enough to maintain the triangle read.

libgraphql-core

pentagon 5 vertices center dot glyph v2

The core engine — schema validation, type system, and operation processing. Its pentagon (5 vertices) sits at mid-high complexity, and the center dot represents the engine nucleus that everything orbits around.

Design Decisions

  • Why pentagon (5)? Core is the most substantial sub-crate, handling schema, types, and operations. 5 vertices places it above macros (4) and cli (4), but below the apex libgraphql (6), reflecting its role as the heavy-lifter that isn't quite the full package.
  • Why center dot? The core is literally the "center" of the ecosystem — everything else depends on it. The dot at the centroid makes this concrete. It's rendered at reduced opacity (0.5 in standalone glyph, 0.85 in banner groups) to remain a secondary accent rather than competing with the polygon outline.
  • Center dot is exclusive to core: This accent was deliberately not added to the libgraphql hexagon to preserve each glyph's unique identity. The hexagon uses interior edges; the pentagon uses the center dot. Different metaphors for different roles.
  • Pointy-top orientation: Consistent with libgraphql's hexagon. The visual centroid of a pointy-top pentagon sits slightly below geometric center, so the translate is offset to (32, 33) / translate(660, 101) in banners.

libgraphql-macros

nested diamonds 4+4 vertices concentric glyph v2

Procedural macros for compile-time schema validation. The nested diamond motif represents "code that generates code" — the outer diamond is the source macro invocation, the inner diamond is the generated/expanded output.

Design Decisions

  • Why nested shapes? Macro expansion is fundamentally about transformation: one thing becomes another thing inside it. Two concentric diamonds at different scales visualize this nesting relationship. The inner diamond represents what the macro produces at compile time.
  • Why diamond (4 vertices)? Diamonds (axis-aligned squares) are mid-low complexity, appropriate for a crate that's an accessory to core rather than the core itself. The 45° rotation distinguishes it from a plain square.
  • Inner diamond proportions: Scaled to 45% of the outer (R=11 vs R=24), with thinner strokes (1.2 vs 1.8 in glyph), smaller vertex dots (r=1.6 vs r=2.3), and reduced opacity (0.55). This makes it clearly secondary — generated, not source.
  • Evolution: v1 was a plain single diamond, which didn't convey the "macro" concept. Nesting was added in v2 and immediately felt right.

libgraphql-cli

flying V 4 vertices directional glyph v2

The command-line interface — a thin wrapper that delivers validation, transformation, and search capabilities to the user. Its right-pointing chevron shape acts as a directional indicator, forming the output-side bookend opposite the parser's input-side gap.

Design Decisions

  • Why flying V (4 vertices)? The CLI is a lightweight delivery mechanism, not a complex engine. 4 vertices keeps it simple. The concave 4th vertex at (-8, 0) in banner coords / (24, 32) in glyph coords creates the inward notch that gives the shape its "arrowhead" character.
  • Why right-pointing? The CLI delivers results to the user. In the processing pipeline, it's the last stage: text enters from the left (parser), gets processed (core), and exits to the right (cli). The directional shape encodes this flow.
  • Evolution: v1 used 5 vertices, which read as an airplane silhouette. Collapsing to 4 vertices cleaned up the shape into a prompt-like chevron that's unmistakably directional.
  • No additional accent needed: The shape's directionality is itself the accent. Unlike the other glyphs that need an interior element (dots, edges, nesting), the CLI's unique contribution is its asymmetric, non-regular polygon silhouette.

5. Banner System

Each crate has two banner variants, both at 900×170 in dark and light SVG files: README Banners and Structural Glyph Banners.

README Banners use a centered composition layout: the crate’s glyph sits centered at the top as a logo seal, with the project name, accent line, subtitle, and metadata stacked beneath it. This creates an unmistakable glyph-to-name association — the universal “icon above wordmark” pattern. README Banners are designed for GitHub README headers using <picture> with prefers-color-scheme media queries.

Structural Glyph Banners place a large 2.2× watermark glyph on the left with text arranged to the right — an editorial, crest-like composition. These are available in assets/structural-glyph-banners/, reserved for future use in documentation, presentations, and social cards.

Compound Opacity in Light Banners

Light banners use reduced group opacity (0.50 for README Banners, 0.20 for Structural Glyph Banners), which compounds with per-element opacity. Elements with their own opacity (ghost vertices, interior diagonals, inner diamonds) can become invisible if their internal opacity is too low. To compensate, light banner SVGs use boosted internal opacities for subtle sub-elements:

ElementDark (README)Light (README)Light effective

core center dot0.850.900.45

Radial Glow

A large radial gradient circle (r=90) sits behind each banner glyph to provide an environmental contrast cushion. On dark backgrounds, it's a white glow (reading as ambient light); on light backgrounds, it's a very subtle dark shadow (reading as a soft vignette). The glow extends well beyond the glyph (which is ~38px radius after scaling), creating an "aura" rather than a hard spotlight.

Grain Texture (Dark Only)

Dark banners include an SVG <filter> chain that generates fractal noise, desaturates it, and composites it at 6% opacity via a multiply blend. This adds subtle analog texture that breaks up the flat gradient. Light banners omit grain entirely — on light backgrounds it reads as dirt.

7. Guidelines for Future Glyphs

Adding a New Crate to the System

  • Assign a vertex count based on the crate's scope and complexity relative to existing crates. The current range is 3–6. New crates should slot into this progression; avoid duplicating an existing crate's exact vertex count unless the shape and accent are clearly different.
  • Choose one unique accent that encodes the crate's role. Refer to the accent vocabulary above and ensure no overlap with existing accents. The accent should be meaningful, not decorative.
  • Use amber only (#d4943c). Do not introduce additional colors. Differentiation comes from geometry alone.
  • Design at 64×64 in a viewBox, then test at 16px and 32px. If the accent is not legible at 16px, simplify or enlarge it. Strokes below 1.2px may not render at favicon sizes.
  • Create dark and light README Banner variants following the README Banner Anatomy table above. Remember to boost internal opacities of subtle elements in light banners to compensate for compound opacity. Optionally also create Structural Glyph Banner variants if the crate will appear in documentation, presentations, or social cards.

Rules to Preserve

  • Vertex count hierarchy must be maintained — higher scope = more vertices
  • Each glyph's accent must remain exclusive to that glyph
  • Parser (left-opening) and CLI (right-pointing) are directional bookends — new crates should not use directional orientation unless they have a clear pipeline-position rationale
  • The center dot belongs to core; interior edges belong to libgraphql; nesting belongs to macros; the ghost vertex belongs to parser
  • Wireframe only — no filled polygons, no gradients within glyphs
  • All glyphs share the same stroke weight system (1.8/1.2/0.7)

File Naming Convention

  • Glyphs: glyph-{crate}.svg (e.g. glyph-parser.svg)
  • Banners: readme-banner-{crate}-{dark|light}.svg (e.g. readme-banner-parser-dark.svg)
  • Version history is tracked via VCS (Sapling), not filename suffixes
  • Edit assets in place — commit history preserves the full evolution
